UK Goods Trade Balance with EU and Non-EU Countries

UK Regional Goods Trade Balance

2016 - 2018

Before Brexit, UK regions showed a clear structural divergence in trade patterns. Most areas had consistent deficits with EU countries, while trade with non-EU partners like the United States and Australia more commonly resulted in surpluses.

2019

Scotland launched its export strategy A Trading Nation, outlining long-term priorities across high-value sectors such as food and drink, energy, life sciences, and advanced manufacturing. With the US and Europe as its main export destinations, the strategy focused on strengthening market support and helping businesses grow their international reach.

2021

The Northern Ireland Protocol came into effect as part of the UK–EU Withdrawal Agreement. It allowed Northern Ireland to remain aligned with EU rules for goods, avoiding a hard border with Ireland. This special arrangement helped maintain smoother trade with EU countries compared to other UK regions.

2022

Trade deficits with EU countries widened across most UK regions, as new customs rules took full effect and long-standing supply chains with Europe came under strain.

2023 - 2024

London has been particularly affected by post-Brexit customs changes. As a key hub for re-exports and high-value goods, the city faced growing trade deficits with EU countries as new border rules disrupted its role in cross-border supply chains.
